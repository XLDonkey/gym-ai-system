Section 01 — The Vision

What Are We Building?

Imagine a person standing in the gym, watching every machine, knowing every member by face, counting every rep, reading every weight — all at once, all the time, never getting tired, never missing anything. That's the AI Overseer.

The goal is simple to say but hard to build: zero friction data capture. A member walks in, sits at a machine, and trains. The AI does everything else. It knows who they are. It knows what machine they're at. It reads the weight. It counts the reps. It scores the form. It logs everything to a database. The member never touches a tablet.

🎯 The North Star

The system should feel like magic to the member. They just train. Behind the scenes, three cameras and four AI models are working in parallel to capture a complete, verified record of their session — better data than any personal trainer could manually collect.

What the System Produces

📊

Verified Session Data

Every set, every rep, every weight — AI-verified. Not self-reported. Not estimated. Actually measured by computer vision.

🏆

Tamper-Proof Leaderboard

Because data is captured automatically, the leaderboard can't be gamed. Every number is real. Trust is restored.

📈

Progress Tracking

Members can see their strength progress over weeks and months. The AI tracks volume, weight progression, and form improvement.

🤖

Coaching Intelligence

Coaches get real data to work with. They can see which members are progressing, who's plateauing, and who needs form correction.

Why This Is Worth Building

Most gyms have a data problem. Members self-report their workouts, and the data is unreliable — people round up, forget, or simply don't log at all. Studies show ~40% of manually logged gym data contains errors. You cannot build a coaching product, a leaderboard, or a personalisation engine on unreliable data.

The AI Overseer solves this at the source. It captures data automatically, at the point of exercise, with no member effort required. This is the foundation for everything else — the leaderboard, the coaching, the personalisation, the business model.

Section 04 — Layer 1: Detection

What is a Bounding Box?

Before the AI can do anything — count reps, identify faces, read weights — it first needs to know where people are in the camera image. A bounding box is the answer.

A bounding box is just a rectangle drawn around a person. That's it. It's defined by four numbers: the x position (left edge), y position (top edge), width, and height — all measured in pixels from the top-left corner of the image.

Look at the image below. Every person has a green rectangle around them and a yellow number. That green rectangle is the bounding box. That yellow number is the tracking ID. This is exactly what our system produces — 30 times per second, for every person on the gym floor.

🎯 Why We Start Here

Every other AI layer depends on bounding boxes. Face ID crops the face from the bounding box. Pose estimation runs inside the bounding box. Zone matching uses the bounding box centre point. Get detection right and everything else follows.

A Bounding Box is Just 4 Numbers

# This is everything YOLO outputs for one detected person

  ],
  "confidence": 0.94,  # 94% sure this is a real person
  "class": "person"    # YOLO detected a person (not a chair, not a weight plate)

Why Not Trace the Exact Outline?

You might wonder: why use a simple rectangle? Why not trace the exact shape of the person? The answer is speed. Tracing exact outlines (called segmentation) takes 10× more computation. A bounding box gives us 95% of the information we need in 1/10th the time.

MethodTime Per FrameInfo QualityOur Choice?
Bounding Box (YOLO)~15msGood — rectangle around person✓ Yes
Pixel Segmentation~150ms+Perfect — exact outline✗ Too slow
Simple Motion Detection~2msPoor — just "something moved"✗ Not enough info
Section 05 — YOLO Detection

YOLO — You Only Look Once

YOLO is the AI model that scans the camera image and finds every person in it. It does this in about 15 milliseconds — fast enough to run 30 times per second on every camera.

The name "You Only Look Once" refers to how it works. Older detection systems would scan an image multiple times, checking different regions at different scales. YOLO scans the entire image in a single forward pass through a neural network. That's why it's fast enough for real-time video.

How YOLO Sees the World

YOLO divides the image into a grid (say, 20×20 cells). For each cell, it predicts: "Is there a person here? If yes, where exactly, and how confident am I?" All of this happens simultaneously across all 400 cells in one pass through the network.

Section 06 — Confidence Scores

What is a Confidence Score?

Every detection YOLO makes comes with a confidence score — a number between 0 and 100% that represents how sure the AI is that it found a real person.

Think of it like a human saying "I'm pretty sure that's a person" vs "I'm absolutely certain that's a person." The AI does the same thing, but with a precise number. A score of 0.94 means the AI is 94% confident it found a real person. A score of 0.31 means it's only 31% sure — probably a false alarm.

Section 07 — Layer 2: Tracking

The Tracking Problem

YOLO detects people in a single frame. But video is a sequence of frames. Without tracking, YOLO has no memory — it sees 30 completely fresh images every second. This creates a critical problem.

Imagine you're watching a security camera. Every second, someone wipes your memory. You see people, but you don't know if the person in front of you is the same person you saw a moment ago. That's YOLO without tracking. It detects people, but it can't follow them.

Why This Matters for the Gym

Without stable tracking IDs, we can't link a person to a machine. We can't count their reps across frames. We can't run face ID (because we don't know which detection to run it on). Stable tracking is the foundation that everything else is built on.

Section 08 — DeepSORT

How DeepSORT Keeps Track

DeepSORT solves the tracking problem using two simultaneous strategies: predict where a person will be next frame, and check if the new detection looks the same as the tracked person. Both must agree for an ID to be maintained.

DeepSORT — 3 Components Explained

Kalman Filter — "Where Will They Be Next Frame?"

A Kalman Filter is a mathematical prediction algorithm. Given a person's current position and velocity, it predicts where they'll be in the next frame. This prediction is used to pre-match new detections before even looking at appearance.

# Kalman Filter — simplified concept
# Current state: where is Person #7 right now?
current_position = (340, 210)
current_velocity = (-2, 0)  # moving 2px left/frame

# Prediction: where will they be NEXT frame?
predicted_next = {
    current_position[0] + current_velocity[0],
    current_position[1] + current_velocity[1]
}
# predicted_next = (338, 210)

# DeepSORT pre-matches detections near (338, 210)
# This dramatically reduces the search space

Appearance Match — "Does This Look Like the Same Person?"

Even if someone is predicted to be at position (338, 210), DeepSORT also checks: does the person at that position look like Person #7? It uses a small neural network to extract a 128-number "appearance descriptor" from each bounding box crop. Similar people have similar descriptors.

Hungarian Algorithm — "Best Global Assignment"

When there are multiple people and multiple detections, we need to find the best overall assignment — which detection belongs to which tracked person. The Hungarian Algorithm solves this optimally in milliseconds.

The Result — Stable IDs Across All Frames

Combining Kalman prediction + appearance matching + Hungarian assignment gives us stable, persistent IDs. Person #7 stays Person #7 whether they're sitting, standing, walking, or partially occluded by equipment.

Section 09 — Zone Matching

Which Machine Is Person #7 At?

We know Person #7 is in the gym. Now we need to know which machine they're at. Zone matching is the bridge between "a person exists" and "a person is at machine #7."

Every machine has a zone — a polygon drawn in camera pixel coordinates. These are rectangles that cover the area where someone would sit when using that machine, as seen from the overhead camera image. If Person #7's bounding box centre point falls inside a zone polygon, they are assigned to that machine.

Section 10 — Layer 3: Pose Estimation

Why Pose Estimation?

We know who is at the machine. Now we need to know what they're doing. A bounding box tells us where a person is — but it doesn't tell us if their arms are extended, if they've completed a rep, or if their form is correct. Pose estimation does.

Pose estimation is the process of finding the location of specific body parts — joints like shoulders, elbows, wrists, hips, knees — in an image. Once we know where the joints are, we can calculate angles, detect movement patterns, and count repetitions.

The Key Insight

A rep is just a joint angle going from one extreme to another and back. Elbow angle goes from 160° (extended) to 40° (contracted) and back to 160°. That's one rep. Pose estimation gives us the joint angles. The LSTM counts the reps.

Section 12 — LSTM Rep Counter

LSTM — The Rep Counter

MoveNet gives us joint positions. But a single frame doesn't tell us if a rep was completed — we need to watch the movement over time. An LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory) neural network is designed exactly for this: it watches a sequence of frames and classifies what happened.

Think of it like this: if you saw a single photo of someone with their arms bent, you couldn't tell if they were at the top or bottom of a curl. But if you watched 30 frames (1 second of video), you could clearly see the full movement. The LSTM does the same thing — it watches 30 frames and outputs a classification.

Section 13 — Layer 4: Identity

Why Face ID?

We know Person #7 is at Machine #4. But who is Person #7? The tracking system gives us a temporary ID number, not a name. Face ID bridges the gap between an anonymous bounding box and a real member profile.

Without face ID, we'd need members to tap a card every time they sit at a machine. With face ID, the system recognises them automatically. The first few sessions might require a card tap to confirm — but after that, the system knows them by face and the session opens instantly.

The Gap We're Closing

Card tap at the door → "Matt is in the building." Face ID at the machine → "Matt is at Machine #7, right now." That second piece of information is what makes the whole system work.

How Face ID Works — 3 Steps

1
Detect the face

YOLO finds the person. We crop the top portion of the bounding box — that's where the face is. We run a face detector to get a tight crop of just the face.

2
Generate an embedding

A face recognition model (InsightFace or DeepFace) converts the face image into a 512-number vector. This is the "mathematical fingerprint" of that face. Similar faces produce similar vectors.

3
Compare to the database

We compare the embedding against all enrolled member embeddings using cosine similarity. The closest match above the confidence threshold is the identified member.

Section 14 — Face Embeddings

Face Embeddings — Mathematical Fingerprints

A face embedding is a list of 512 numbers that represents a face. The AI model is trained so that the same person always produces similar numbers, and different people always produce very different numbers.

You don't need to understand what each number means — the model figures that out during training. What matters is: same person = similar vector. Different person = different vector. We measure similarity using cosine similarity — a number between 0 and 1, where 1 means identical.

Section 17 — Layer 5: Weight Detection

Weight Stack AI

The third camera faces the weight stack directly. A computer vision model reads which pin is inserted and calculates the loaded weight. No manual input. No guessing.

Weight stacks have numbered plates. The selector pin is inserted between plates to choose the weight. The camera reads the number on the plate just above the pin. That number, multiplied by the plate weight increment, gives the loaded weight.

Why LSTM Over a Simple Classifier?

A simple classifier (MLP) looks at a single frame and asks "what is happening right now?" An LSTM looks at a sequence of 30 frames and asks "what happened over the last second?" For rep counting, you need the sequence — a single frame can't tell you if a rep was completed.

# LSTM input: 30 frames × 34 features per frame
# Features = 17 keypoints × 2 (x, y) = 34 numbers per frame
# So the LSTM sees: 30 × 34 = 1,020 numbers total

sequence = [
  [nose_x, nose_y, left_shoulder_x, left_shoulder_y, ...],  # frame 1
  [nose_x, nose_y, left_shoulder_x, left_shoulder_y, ...],  # frame 2
  # ... 28 more frames
  [nose_x, nose_y, left_shoulder_x, left_shoulder_y, ...],  # frame 30
]

# Output: one of 7 classes
output = lstm.predict(sequence)
# → {"good_rep": 0.94, "partial_rep": 0.03, "rest": 0.02, ...}

Section 36 — Deep Dive: Member Journey

The Member Journey

From the member's perspective, the system is invisible. They just train. Here's what happens behind the scenes at every step.

1
Member taps card at the door

GymMaster logs entry. The AI system is notified: "Matt McArdle is in the building." Face ID is now on high alert for Matt's face.

2
Member walks to Machine #7

Camera 1 detects them as Person #14. DeepSORT assigns a stable tracking ID. Zone matching assigns them to Machine #7.

3
Face ID runs

Camera 1 crops the face region. InsightFace generates an embedding. System matches to Matt McArdle at 0.97 confidence. Session opens automatically.

4
Camera 3 reads the weight

Weight stack AI detects the selector pin at plate 8. Machine is configured for 5kg increments. Weight = 40kg. Logged to Supabase.

5
Matt starts his set

Camera 2 runs MoveNet on every frame. LSTM watches the sequence. After 10 reps, it classifies: 10 × good_rep. Tablet updates in real-time.

6
Matt rests between sets

LSTM classifies state as "rest". Rest timer starts. System waits for next set to begin.

7
Matt leaves the machine

Zone matching detects Matt has left Machine #7. Session closes. Total volume calculated. Leaderboard updated. Session summary sent to Matt's profile.

Section 37 — Deep Dive: The AI System

The AI System

Four AI subsystems work in parallel. Each runs on its own thread. The orchestrator combines their outputs every frame.

📦

Person Tracking

YOLO + DeepSORT. Runs on Camera 1 stream. Outputs: person ID, bounding box, machine zone assignment. 30fps.

🦾

Rep Counting

MoveNet + LSTM. Runs on Camera 2 stream. Outputs: rep count, form score, current state. 30fps.

⚖️

Weight Detection

OCR + position model. Runs on Camera 3 stream. Outputs: weight_kg, confidence. Runs every 5 seconds (weight doesn't change mid-set).

👤

Face ID

InsightFace. Runs on Camera 1 stream. Outputs: member_id, confidence. Runs every 2 seconds (face doesn't change).

Section 39 — Deep Dive: Training Labels

Training Labels

The labelling system divides the gym into sections. Each section has its own labelling queue. Donkey and staff label sequences during quiet periods.

1
Camera 2 records a session

All raw video is saved locally. We don't need to label in real-time.

2
Video is segmented into 1-second clips

Each clip is one potential rep or rest period. Clips are added to the labelling queue.

3
Labeller opens the web tool

Clip plays. Labeller presses: G (good rep), P (partial rep), B (bad form), R (rest), S (setup), U (unknown).

4
Label is saved to Supabase

Clip + label + timestamp + machine_id stored. Used for LSTM training.

5
Model retrained weekly

Every Sunday, Manus runs the training script. New model deployed automatically.
