2. The Current Neural Network (MLP v3)

What it is

An MLP (Multi-Layer Perceptron) is the simplest form of neural network. It takes a fixed set of summary numbers and outputs a classification. Think of it as a very sophisticated if/then rule that learned its thresholds from data.

What it looks at (5 features per rep)

Minimum Angle

How far down the bar was pulled. Good reps reach ~13°, bad reps ~23°, false movements ~64°.

Range of Motion (ROM)

Total angle swept during the rep. Good reps ~155°, false movements ~88°.

Duration

How long the rep took in seconds. The single most predictive feature (60.9% importance).

Average Angle

The mean elbow angle throughout the rep.

Standard Deviation

How much the angle varied — captures smoothness vs jerkiness.

Key Limitation
The MLP summarises an entire rep into 5 numbers and throws away the time dimension. It cannot see HOW the movement happened — only what the summary statistics are. This means it struggles to distinguish a controlled good rep from a fast bad rep with similar ROM. The LSTM fixes this.

3. The Upgrade: LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory)

What is an LSTM?

An LSTM is a type of neural network designed specifically for sequences over time. Instead of receiving 5 summary numbers, it receives the entire rep as a stream of data — one reading per frame — and learns the temporal patterns that define good form.

What LSTM can detect that MLP cannot

⚡ Speed Profile

Good reps have a controlled pull and slower return. Too fast = bad form. LSTM sees the velocity curve, MLP cannot.

🌊 Smoothness

Jerky, bouncing reps (using momentum) have a characteristic jitter signature frame-by-frame. LSTM detects it.

📐 Symmetry

Uneven pulls where one side does more work. Detectable from left vs right arm angle divergence over time.

🔒 Machine Contact

Real lat pulldown motion has a cable-constrained arc that differs subtly from free-air motion.

What to record (3 video categories)

CategoryWhat to filmKey characteristics
Good Reps Full range of motion, controlled tempo, back straight, bar pulled to chin/chest Min angle <20°, ROM >130°, smooth velocity curve, 3–5s per rep
Bad Reps Partial pulls (only halfway down), swinging/momentum, leaning forward excessively, jerky movement, too fast Min angle 20–70°, ROM <100°, OR jittery velocity pattern
False Movements Sitting without pulling, adjusting weight stack, reaching forward, arm waves, stretching, talking to someone Low ROM (<80°), wrists not starting overhead, short duration
Recording Tips (Critical for Data Quality)

Camera angle: Side-on (90° to machine). Full body visible. Shoulder, elbow, and wrist must all be in frame.

Lighting: Even, well-lit. Avoid strong backlight from windows. Gym overhead lighting is fine.

Multiple people: Each person sits slightly differently. More people = more robust model.

Breaks are fine: Gaps between reps help the model learn the transition between reps and idle state.
